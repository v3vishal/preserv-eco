Here, to calculate the amount of Tigers that could have benefitted by the amount of trees lost, the following was done;

  • As per the 10% law of energy transfer efficiency, with an assumption of 1000 Joules of energy absorption by a tree on 1,00,000 joules of sun energy that would fall on the surface of Earth, that translates to 1 Joule of energy transferred to a tiger, where the tiger is the tertiary consumer. With an estimated 5000 Joules of energy consumption for a tiger per day, this would mean that around 5000 trees would be needed to sustain the energy requirement of a tiger per day.

    • Therefore, a loss of 12 to 15 lakhs of trees would have indirectly (via the food chain) meant that the energy requirement for around 250 to 300 tigers would have been impacted.

    Presently, we have a very small number of tigers. The current extent of forestation and tree density is essential to maintain and sustain the current population. Any further reduction will not only create pressure on the existing ecosystem but also limit further expansion of the tiger population.     Impact on Humans and Society:

    The imbalance in trophic levels and the disruption of the ecosystem have profound impacts on humans and our society. As integral parts of the food web, we are interconnected with the well-being of other species.

    Decreased tiger population, driven by the loss of trees and disruption in the food chain, can lead to an increase in certain species that tigers would typically control. This imbalance could result in overpopulation of herbivores, affecting vegetation and potentially leading to increased human-wildlife conflicts as herbivores encroach on human settlements in search of food.

    Additionally, invasive species can further exacerbate the situation. The spread of invasive plants and herbivores can disrupt local ecosystems, affecting agriculture, water resources, and even human health.

    It is crucial for us to recognize our role in the ecosystem and strive for a balanced coexistence. Conservation efforts not only benefit wildlife but also contribute to the overall health and sustainability of our environment, directly impacting the quality of life for us and future generations.
